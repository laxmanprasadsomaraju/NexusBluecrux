1. The problem — grounded in Bluecrux&apos;s own client evidence
Every case study on Bluecrux&apos;s website tells the same story from a different angle. The tools exist. The data exists. But the moment a problem surfaces, teams fall apart. Here is what actually happened to Bluecrux&apos;s real clients:
AstraZeneca — QC had no seat at the table
Real client quote (AstraZeneca Director of QC Capacity &amp; Modelling):&quot;QC always wanted a seat at the table in supply consensus meetings and we could go but we weren&apos;t getting any value from them because we didn&apos;t have the capability to show what our capacities were.&quot;What happened: QC lab teams were using manual Excel reports. By the time they spotted a capacity problem, Supply Chain had already made decisions. Nobody&apos;s fault — there was no shared, real-time view and no governed process for QC to raise an issue and get it actioned.
Argenx (biotech) — no coordination across CMO partners
Real pain (from Argenx case study):&quot;Difficulties in aligning supply chain planning across different regions and CMO partners. Time-intensive and complex to run what-if scenarios. No integration between long-term network planning and order management.&quot;What happened: A biotech company with a €2.2B revenue had no systematic way to flag, escalate and resolve supply problems across their external manufacturing network. Each issue became a fire drill handled over email.
Maesa (beauty/CPG) — high inventory, no alerting, all manual
Real pain (from Maesa case study):&quot;High inventory due to difficulty in balancing cash, cost &amp; service. Complex planning environment with a mix of turnkey and non-turnkey. Desire to move away from manual planning.&quot;What happened: No exception alerting meant problems were discovered late. Planners were doing everything manually — reactive, not proactive.
Clariant Catalysts — opportunities hidden, no structured escalation
Real pain (from Clariant case study):&quot;The main challenge centred on the perception that there were limited opportunities to reduce inventory.&quot; Bluecrux had to run a 4-month diagnostic just to surface what the problems were.What happened: Nobody had a systematic view of what exceptions existed, who owned them, and what impact they were having. Decisions were made on assumptions, not facts.
The common thread across every case
Every client had good tools — SAP, Anaplan, Axon, Helion. But they all suffered from the same gap: when the tool detected a problem, nobody had a governed process to:
See the exception clearly with business impact attached
Know who owns it and what action they need to take
Track whether it got resolved and how long it took
Report to leadership on value recovered vs value at risk
This is not a data problem. Bluecrux&apos;s tools already surface the data. This is an execution and coordination problem — and that is exactly what NEXUS solves.
2. What is NEXUS?
NEXUS is a Supply Chain Exception and Escalation Hub. In plain English:
One-line description:NEXUS automatically catches supply chain problems from Axon, Helion, Anaplan, Binocs and SAP — then turns each one into an assigned, tracked, time-bound action with a named owner, a deadline, an AI-suggested resolution, and a live audit trail — so nothing falls through the cracks.
What it is NOT
Not another dashboard — Axon already does dashboards brilliantly
Not a replacement for Axon, Helion, Binocs or Anaplan — it sits on top of them
Not a generic project management tool — it is purpose-built for supply chain exceptions
Not an AI that makes decisions — it suggests, humans approve, everything is auditable
Who uses it
Persona
What they do in NEXUS
Supply Chain Planning Lead
Reviews the exception queue every morning. Approves or delegates actions. Tracks how long teams are taking to respond.
QC / Lab Operations Manager
Gets notified immediately when a batch hold creates a supply risk. Provides resolution update directly in NEXUS — no more email chains.
External Manufacturing Manager
Manages CMO capacity exceptions. Sends capacity requests to partners through the tool and tracks their response time.
Supply Chain Director / VP
Opens the Executive View. Sees total value at risk, team response times, and resolution trends — in one screen, without chasing updates.
Plant / Site Manager
Receives assigned actions for site-specific exceptions. Logs progress and closes them when resolved.
3. Full feature specification
Feature 1 — Exception queue (the nerve centre)
This is the first screen every user sees when they log in. It is the single source of truth for all active supply chain problems.
What it shows:
All open exceptions, pulled automatically from Axon, Helion, Anaplan, Binocs and SAP
Each exception shows: severity level (Critical / High / Medium), source system, assigned owner, age (how long it has been open), current status
Four summary stat cards at the top: Critical open, Awaiting action, Resolved this week, Average time to act
Filter options (click to filter):
All — shows everything
Critical — only red severity
Mine — only exceptions assigned to the logged-in user
Partner — only exceptions involving CMOs, suppliers or 3PLs
What each click does:
Click any exception card → Opens the full detail panel on the right. Shows impact analysis, activity timeline, AI suggestion, and action buttons.
&quot;Raise exception&quot; button → Opens a form modal. User enters: title, severity, source system, owner, and notes. On submit, exception appears in queue and owner gets notified.
&quot;Sync&quot; button → Manually triggers a data pull from connected source systems.
Severity dot (red/amber/blue) → Visual indicator — red = critical, amber = high, blue = medium. Colour-coded consistently throughout the tool.
Feature 2 — Exception detail panel
When a user clicks any exception card, the right panel loads the full context. This replaces the current reality of digging through emails, Axon dashboards and Teams messages.
Sections in the detail panel:
Impact analysis — Detected by (which system), business impact in currency (e.g. €1.2M revenue at risk), affected volume, risk date, current owner, escalation path
AI suggestion box — A cyan-bordered panel showing what the AI recommends and its confidence score (e.g. &apos;Re-slot batch to Thu AM — confidence 87%&apos;). This is always clearly labelled as a suggestion, never an automatic action.
Activity timeline — A chronological log of everything that has happened: when Axon detected it, when the owner acknowledged it, what actions have been taken, what partner responses came in. Immutable — cannot be deleted.
Partner status panel — Appears for CMO/supplier exceptions. Shows when a request was sent to the partner, whether they acknowledged it, and auto-escalation countdown.
Action buttons at the bottom:
Approve [action] — Confirms the AI-suggested or proposed action. Logs it in the timeline with user name and timestamp. Pushes the decision back to the source system where applicable (e.g. confirms re-slot in Binocs).
Escalate — Opens a form to escalate to the next level in the escalation path. User can add a note and set a deadline.
Add note — Opens a text input to log a comment into the timeline. Useful for adding context or updating status.
Open in [source system] — Deep link that opens the relevant record directly in Axon, Helion, Binocs or SAP. Saves the user having to navigate manually.
Feature 3 — My actions
A personal to-do list — not a generic task manager, but a supply-chain-specific action list showing only what this user needs to act on today.
What each action card shows:
Status badge: Overdue (red) / Due today (amber) / Tomorrow (blue) / This week (gray)
Action title — specific and actionable (e.g. &apos;Approve Binocs re-slot for Batch X-4421&apos;)
Context: which exception it comes from, severity level, due time
One-click action button (Approve / Review / Start) right on the card — no need to navigate to the exception first
Sort order:
Overdue items always at top
Then due today, ordered by deadline time
Then tomorrow, then this week
Feature 4 — Executive view
Designed for Supply Chain Directors and VPs. One screen that answers the question: &apos;How are we doing?&apos; — without needing to ask anyone.
Cards and charts on this screen:
Exception resolution trend — A bar chart showing open vs resolved exceptions over the last 4 weeks. Leadership can see immediately whether things are getting better or worse.
Exceptions by source system — Horizontal bar chart showing how many exceptions are coming from Axon vs Helion vs Anaplan vs manual. Helps identify systemic issues.
Team response times — Bar chart by team (QC, SC Planning, External Mfg, Procurement) showing average time from exception opened to first action. Red = slow, green = fast. Makes accountability visible.
Value at risk — Total € value of all open exceptions combined, plus the biggest single contributor. Compared to last week to show trend.
Actions completed on time — Percentage of actions that were completed before their deadline. Target line shown (e.g. 85%). Compared to last month.
Export PDF button — One click generates a PDF version of the executive view for board/leadership reporting.
Feature 5 — Partner network view
Shows all CMOs, suppliers and 3PLs in a simple table. Designed for External Manufacturing Managers and Procurement teams who need to know which partner is causing problems right now.
Columns:
Partner name
Type (CMO / Supplier / 3PL)
Open exceptions — how many and what severity
Response time — average hours from request sent to partner response
Status badge — On track / Monitoring / Needs action
What clicking a partner row does:
Filters the main exception queue to show only that partner&apos;s exceptions
Shows partner scorecard with trend over last 90 days
Feature 6 — Raise exception modal (manual entry)
Any user can raise an exception manually — for situations not yet detected by the connected systems. Designed to take under 60 seconds.
Form fields:
Exception title (free text)
Severity (dropdown: Critical / High / Medium)
Source system (dropdown: Axon / Helion / Anaplan / SAP / Manual)
Owner (search by name or team)
Notes / context (free text area)
What happens on submit:
Exception appears immediately in the queue
Assigned owner receives an in-app notification
Timeline entry is automatically created: &apos;Exception raised manually by [user name] at [time]&apos;
4. Full end-to-end user flows
Flow A — QC batch hold (AstraZeneca scenario)
This is the most critical flow and the one that directly addresses the AstraZeneca pain point where QC had no way to communicate capacity issues to supply chain.
Step
What happens
Step 1
Axon detects that Batch X-4421 has entered a QC hold at Site 3. It estimates the EU release window is at risk by 3 days. This triggers an automatic exception in NEXUS.
Step 2
NEXUS classifies it as Critical (QC hold + release window risk). It assigns it to Eric R. (QC Ops Lead) based on routing rules. Eric gets an in-app notification.
Step 3
Eric opens NEXUS. He sees the exception in his queue and clicks it. The detail panel shows: batch number, release date risk, estimated €1.2M revenue impact, and Binocs data showing analyst availability.
Step 4
The AI suggestion box recommends: &apos;Re-slot batch to Thu AM with analyst Müller — projected release Nov 13, within window. Confidence 87%.&apos;
Step 5
Eric reviews the suggestion, agrees, and clicks &apos;Approve re-slot&apos;. NEXUS logs his approval with name and timestamp. It pushes the reschedule instruction to Binocs.
Step 6
Binocs confirms the re-slot. NEXUS updates the exception status to &apos;Action taken — monitoring&apos;. The timeline entry is added automatically.
Step 7
Supply Chain Director opens the Executive View the next morning. She sees the exception moved from &apos;Critical open&apos; to &apos;Resolved&apos;. Resolution time: 6.2 hours. Value protected: €1.2M.
Flow B — CMO capacity shortfall (Argenx/biotech scenario)
Directly addresses the Argenx pain point: no coordination across CMO partners, no way to flag and escalate supply gaps.
Step
What happens

7. Success metrics — how to know it is working
These are the metrics Bluecrux should track from day one. They are based on the language Bluecrux already uses in its own case studies and product pages — inventory reduction, lead-time reduction, hours saved, cost avoidance.
Operational metrics (measured inside the tool)
Metric
What it measures and target
Average time to first action
How long from exception detected to first owner action. Target: under 4 hours for critical. Baseline today: typically 1–3 days via email.
Exception resolution rate
Percentage of exceptions resolved within their target window. Target: 80%+ within first 6 months.
Actions completed on time
Percentage of assigned actions completed before their deadline. Target: 85%. This is the headline KPI for the executive view.
AI suggestion acceptance rate
How often users approve the AI-recommended action vs overriding it. Target: 60%+ acceptance — if lower, suggests suggestions need tuning.
Manual exceptions raised
Volume of exceptions raised manually vs auto-detected. High manual = detection gaps in source systems. Low manual = tool is trusted.
Business value metrics (reported in executive view)
Metric
What it measures
Value at risk — open
Total € value of unresolved exceptions. Should decrease over time as resolution rate improves.
Value protected — resolved
Total € value of exceptions resolved before they caused a business impact. This is the commercial case for the tool.
Partner response latency
Average hours for CMOs and suppliers to respond to capacity requests. Directly addressable by the partner network view.
Escalations avoided
How many exceptions were resolved at owner level before needing to escalate. Reduces leadership burden.
Hours saved per week
Estimated hours removed from manual chasing, email threads and status meetings. Easy to self-report in quarterly reviews.
